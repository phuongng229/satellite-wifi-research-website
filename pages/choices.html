Satellite Communication Choices

What options do underserved communties have?

01

Radio Frequency vs Optical Communications

With satellite Wi-Fi, there are not only choices in providers, but also choices in the type of communication technology. Radio frequency (RF) and optical (laser) communications are the main methods of satellite communication, but both options come with trade-offs (NASA, 2026a).

RF communications transmit data using electromagnetic radio waves through antennas. These signals can travel through buildings and complex geographical landscapes and offer reliable communications through atmospheric conditions like clouds, rain and fog (Pranavnath, 2026). But they are restricted in the amount of data that can be sent at one time, due to the limited bandwidth of electromagnetic waves (Pranavnath, 2026).

The other option is Optical communications which use the light spectrum to transfer data rather than radio waves. It has a greater bandwidth, allowing for more data to be transferred, but at the cost of needing extremely precise positioning. With it also being highly affect by weather conditions like clouds and rain (Manning & Schauer, 2023).

02

Implementation of Satellite Communications for underserved communities

In remote underserved communities there are often challenges of varying terrain, weather conditions and spread-out housing. Because of this is optical communications aren't a suitable option, as they are less reliable in poor weather and require extremely precise alignment between transmitter and receiver (NASA, 2026a). In contrast, RF communications are more reliable under varying environmental conditions and are less dependent on precise alignment (NASA, 2026a). Making them the best option for satellite Wi-Fi, especially in remote areas, as they are able to cover wider areas.

There are also the choices regarding the distance satellites are located from Earth. The two main options are geostationary orbit (GEO) and low-earth orbit (LEO) satellites. GEO satellites are positioned over the equator around 36,000 km above Earth, which allows for wide area and steady coverage, but at the cost of high latency (McMahon et al. 2025). LEO satellites sit much lower, around 500km up, offering a lower latency connection but lacking the wide area coverage. This shortcoming is made up for by optical communication links, which are used to connect satellites creating a constellation-like network. Allowing for wider area coverage and improved data transfer between satellites (NASA, 2026b).

03

What Satellite system is best for underserved communities?

LEO satellite systems appear to be the most suitable option for underserved communities due to their lower latency and higher speed. They can support modern internet applications such as online education, video conferencing and remote work.

However, these benefits come with higher infrastructure and subscription costs. One example being the Nishnawbe Aski Nation spending $12 million over a 3-year-period on emergency Starlink services (McMahon et al. 2025). To address these challenges, the research done by McMahon et al. (2025) found two business models available in remote communities. The first is a direct-to-home model, like Starlink, where internet is sent directly to each house, but gives all the control to the satellite company. With this model, the satellite company controls everything, the equipment, pricing and how the internet is managed. The other option is the community aggregator model, which gives the locals control, with one connection point that is distributed throughout the community. This comes with potential limitations, as depending on the service, you wouldn't be getting the newest and best technology. Additionally, managing the system would require someone of technical expertise, which may not always be an option in remote communities.

GEO systems remain useful for wide-area public connectivity in remote regions, as shown by Brazil's SGDC program (Global Infrastructure Hub, 2021). Which has connected thousands of schools, health stations, libraries, and indigenous facilities across difficult-to-reach regions. GEO systems are well suited for fixed installations and wider area coverage, but their higher latency makes them less suitable for real-time communication services (McMahon et al. 2025).

Therefore, the best implementation choice for underserved communities depends on their needs and economic standings. Balancing affordability and performance with the specific needs of each community is the best path forward.
