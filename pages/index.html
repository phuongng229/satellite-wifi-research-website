Satellite
Wi-Fi

Satellite Wi-Fi provides an alternative global connectivity solution for users. This technology is well-known for delivering high-speed internet directly from low Earth orbit (LEO). As it doesn’t require underground cables, it is being used across settings ranging from remote classrooms to disaster zones that traditional infrastructure has long left behind. However, this technology also raises serious questions about security, data privacy, the environment, and equity.

Abstract

Satellite Wi-Fi is not a new technology in our modern era, but it has emerged as one of the most transformative connectivity technologies across both industry and academia, due to its potential to address one of the most significant challenges in our time: global connectivity. The emergence of low Earth orbit (LEO) satellite systems promises to reduce service costs compared to traditional connection methods, while still bringing meaningful improvements in latency, bandwidth, and geographic coverage — reaching remote areas that traditional infrastructure has consistently failed to serve.

On the one hand, many opportunities presented by satellite Wi-Fi are substantial and well-documented. Across education, disaster response, healthcare, and economic participation, LEO satellite systems have demonstrated their capacity to deliver meaningful connectivity to remote and underserved populations worldwide, in places where cable-based broadband was never a realistic prospect. On the other hand, the rapid scaling of satellite deployments introduces serious risks that are not always visible to everyday users or adequately addressed by current governance frameworks. Cybersecurity vulnerabilities arising from the open nature of satellite signals, privacy concerns linked to the concentration of global communications within a single private provider, environmental consequences spanning ecological damage and orbital debris accumulation, and affordability gaps that may deepen rather than close the digital divide — all demand urgent and honest attention.

In this project, we present a comprehensive assessment of satellite Wi-Fi, examining both its opportunities and risks across technological, social, environmental, and ethical dimensions. For each area, we highlight key findings, ethical implications, and concrete recommendations, ultimately arguing that realising the full potential of satellite Wi-Fi requires not only technological ambition, but responsible, equitable, and transparent deployment.

Introduction

Satellite Wi-Fi, particularly through low Earth orbit (LEO) constellations such as Starlink, has rapidly emerged as one of the most transformative connectivity technologies of the modern era. For decades, traditional broadband infrastructure — dependent on physical cables, towers, and ground-based relay systems — has left billions of people in remote, rural, and poor regions without reliable internet access. Satellite Wi-Fi changes that equation fundamentally, delivering connectivity directly from orbit and bypassing the geographic and economic barriers that have made conventional infrastructure impossible to build across mountains, islands, and sparsely populated plains.

The scale of the problem satellite Wi-Fi aims to solve is significant. Approximately 2.6 billion people worldwide remain offline as of 2024 — not because the internet does not exist, but because reaching them through ground-based infrastructure is prohibitively expensive. Low Earth orbit systems have overcome this barrier by achieving performance levels that make satellite internet genuinely useful rather than merely technically present: latency as low as 20 milliseconds and download speeds between 100 and 250 Mbps, compared to the 600-millisecond delays that plagued earlier geostationary satellite systems. In education, this has already made a tangible difference — satellite connectivity has reached tens of thousands of students across Nigeria, Rwanda, Malawi, and Indigenous communities in the United States, providing access to digital learning tools in places where a reliable connection was previously unimaginable. In healthcare, it has enabled virtual consultations and remote diagnostics for patients in rural areas who would otherwise struggle to access specialist medical services. In disaster response, satellite terminals have proven critical for restoring communication when earthquakes, floods, and conflict destroy ground-based networks overnight.

However, these opportunities do not exist in isolation. The same technology that connects a remote classroom in Malawi or restores communication after an earthquake also introduces risks that are serious, growing, and frequently underacknowledged. The open nature of satellite signals creates structural cybersecurity vulnerabilities that traditional cable-based infrastructure does not face. The concentration of global communications within a single private provider raises difficult questions about data privacy, corporate power, and political accountability. The environmental footprint of manufacturing, launching, and eventually deorbiting thousands of satellites — including the threat of Kessler Syndrome destabilising Earth's orbital environment — presents consequences that extend far beyond the communities satellite Wi-Fi serves. And the hardware costs, performance inconsistencies, and lack of local technical support that persist in the most remote regions risk ensuring that the communities most in need benefit the least.

This project critically examines all of these dimensions — opportunities, risks, and ethical choices — to provide a balanced, evidence-based assessment of satellite Wi-Fi, and to identify what truly responsible deployment must look like going forward.
